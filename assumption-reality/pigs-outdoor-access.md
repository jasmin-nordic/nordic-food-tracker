# Assumption vs Reality: Organic pigs and outdoor access

**Label:** EU Organic / Danish Ø-label  
**Topic:** Outdoor access for pigs  
**Last verified:** April 2026

---

## Common assumption

> "Organic means pigs live outdoors most of the time."

---

## Where the gap is

| What people assume | What the standard requires |
|---|---|
| Pigs spend most of their time outside | Outdoor access must be available |
| "Outdoor" means pasture or grass | Outdoor runs are often concrete yards |
| Time outside is monitored | Only the existence of access is checked |

---

## Reality

Organic rules require that pigs have **access** to outdoor areas — but the regulation does not specify how much time pigs must actually spend outside.

In practice, Danish organic growing-finishing pigs are typically **housed indoors** with access to a concrete outdoor yard. Research on Danish organic pig production confirms this as the standard system: sows are kept on pasture year-round, but growing and finishing pigs are generally kept indoors with outdoor yard access — not living outside.

Whether a pig uses the outdoor area, and for how long, depends on weather, season, the design of the outdoor run, and individual animal behaviour. None of these are specified or audited by the regulation.

---

## What the regulation actually says

**EU Organic Regulation 2018/848, Annex II, Part II** requires that organic livestock housing conditions ensure animals' developmental, physiological, and ethological needs are met, and that animals have access to outdoor spaces.

The regulation specifies **minimum space allowances** (indoor and outdoor square metres per pig) but does not set a minimum number of hours per day that pigs must spend outdoors.

The Danish Ø-label operates under the same EU organic framework. Annual on-farm inspections verify that outdoor access exists — not that it is used for a defined portion of the day.

## Notes

- This file covers growing-finishing pigs. Sow outdoor access rules differ.
- "Outdoor access" in EU organic rules includes concrete yards — it does not require grass or pasture for pigs (unlike some private standards).
- This assumption/reality gap is one of the most commonly misunderstood aspects of organic pig labels across consumer surveys.
